Civic Lab

From online outrage
to civic infrastructure

India has no shortage of people pointing out what's broken. What it lacks is structured, public, durable evidence of those failures — the kind that a municipal office, a journalist, or a court can actually use. The Civic Lab is The Bengal Reader's effort to build small, open tools that turn scattered complaints into datasets, and datasets into accountability.

Why this exists

From slacktivism to civic infrastructure

The case for building tools instead of just posting about the problem.

India has one of the largest populations of young, online, and chronically under-employed people in the world. A great deal of that energy goes into commentary — threads, reels, and posts that name a problem, attract attention for a news cycle, and then disappear. The criticism is often accurate. It rarely outlives the algorithm that carried it.

That same population — educated, digitally fluent, and looking for meaningful work — is also exactly the workforce needed to build something more durable: a distributed civic auditing system. Not a political party, not a protest movement with a leader to arrest, but a set of open tools that do quietly and permanently what a viral post does loudly and briefly — document a failure, attach evidence, and make it impossible to pretend it didn't happen.

A government can ignore a tweet. It is much harder to ignore a structured, geo-tagged, time-stamped dataset showing the same failure repeated five thousand times across a district — especially once that dataset has already been sent to the journalists and officials responsible for it. That is the shift this lab is built around: from individual outrage to collective, verifiable record-keeping.

There is also a practical upside that has nothing to do with politics. Every civic-tech tool needs people to build and maintain it — frontend developers, data analysts, legal researchers, journalists, video editors, project managers. Contributing to a real, public, open-source project produces a portfolio that a meme account never will: shipped features, merged pull requests, published datasets, and a track record a recruiter or a newsroom can verify directly. Civic participation and skill-building are not separate goals here — they reinforce each other.

None of this requires a registered organisation, a headquarters, or a single leader. It requires open repositories, public data, and a small number of people willing to keep the lights on. That is what the Civic Lab is: not a campaign, but infrastructure.

The charter

What we stand for

The principles below started life as a half-joke "party constitution." Stripped of the joke, they are the operating rules we actually hold ourselves to — and the standards we think any civic-accountability project should meet.

Principle 01

Resilience over confrontation

Civic projects that depend on a single account, a single server, or a single visible leader are easy to suppress — by trolling, by takedown requests, or simply by the news cycle moving on. Tools and datasets that are open-source, mirrored, and maintained by a distributed group survive pressure that a single voice cannot.

Build things that outlast the news cycle that inspired them.

Principle 02

Civic work as livelihood

Skilled, under-employed people are not a liability to a civic movement — they are its workforce. Anyone who has the technical skills to build a public dataset, an RTI pipeline, or a reporting tool, and the time to do it, has something genuinely useful to contribute, with or without a job title.

Under-employment is not a disqualifier. It is the available capacity.

Principle 03

Public data is the foundation

Every claim this lab makes should be traceable to a public record — a tender document, an RTI response, a court filing, an official press release, or a geo-tagged photograph with metadata. Opinions are cheap. Verifiable evidence is the product.

If it can't be sourced, it doesn't go in the dataset.

Editorial standards

The code of conduct

These rules govern how this lab and its contributors critique people in power. They are not optional, and they exist to keep the work both legally sound and morally defensible.

Standard The rule Why it matters
Policy over person Critique the policy, the system, and the institutional failure. Avoid targeting a politician's personal or family life. Policy critique is a protected right. Personal attacks invite defamation liability and shift attention away from the substance of the failure being documented.
No hate speech Zero tolerance for communal, casteist, sexist, or otherwise discriminatory language. We hold power accountable; we do not target marginalised communities. Hate speech destroys credibility instantly and undermines the case being made. Accountability work that punches down is not accountability work.
Verify before publishing Every claim must be backed by a real, citable source. Cross-check figures against primary documents before they go into any dataset or article. A single fabricated or exaggerated claim can be used to discredit an entire body of otherwise accurate work. Accuracy is the only defence against bad-faith dismissal.
No incitement Documentation and publication only — never calls for violence, riots, or unlawful action. The tools here are for recording and reporting, not organising confrontation. Public order is not the target; informed scrutiny is. Calls to action that cross into incitement put contributors at legal risk and undermine the project's legitimacy.

Independence

Funding and editorial independence

The Civic Lab takes no corporate or political funding, and holds no central treasury. What individual contributors do to support themselves is their own business — with one firm line.

Encouraged

Independent, transparent income

Contributors are free to build their own audiences, newsletters, freelance work, or paid projects built on the skills and portfolio they develop here. Independent income is not a conflict of interest — it's proof that civic-skill-building has real economic value.

The lab itself does not take a cut, does not require exclusivity, and does not gate contributions behind paid tiers. The code, data, and methodology stay open regardless of who built them.

Not acceptable

Paid silence

Accepting money — from any party, politician, corporation, or intermediary — in exchange for softening, removing, or suppressing a specific finding is a breach of the one rule that actually matters here: the data goes where the evidence points, regardless of who would prefer otherwise.

Anyone found to have done this is removed from the project and their contributions are flagged publicly in the relevant repository's history. There is no quiet way to walk that back.

Operating model

How a small, open project stays standing

None of these tools depend on a registered organisation or a single point of failure. The model is deliberately boring: public data, open code, and distribution to the people who can act on it.

The operations stack

Layer 1 — Idea & coordination

Open community channels — no central leadership, no registered entity, just contributors proposing and discussing what to build next.

↓

Layer 2 — Build & maintenance

Public repositories under open licences. The codebase is the product — anyone can fork it, and no single person or group can lock others out.

↓

Layer 3 — Output & distribution

Published findings go to the public site and, where relevant, directly to the local authorities, journalists, and oversight bodies the data concerns.

Note 01

Anonymity for contributors, transparency for the work

Individual contributors can remain pseudonymous — that's normal practice on open-source projects and protects people from harassment. But the code, the datasets, and the methodology are entirely public, regardless of who wrote them.

The work is transparent even when the worker isn't. Anyone can audit the methodology; nobody has to put their name on a target list to contribute.

Note 02

Sourced strictly from public records

By sticking to public data — RTI responses, government expenditure reports, tender documents, official press releases, and citizen-submitted evidence with verifiable metadata — the work stays squarely in the territory of public-interest journalism and civic record-keeping.

This is the government's own data, organised and made legible. That is a public service, not a campaign against anyone.

What we've built

From principles to prototypes

Two things have come out of this lab so far: a set of small civic-tech demos built around the principles above, and a separate, larger experimental project exploring how friction in everyday bureaucratic processes can be measured and visualised.

Prototype suite

Civic Tech Demos

A consolidated preview of three early-stage civic-tech tools built around the ideas in this lab:

  • A civic-failure registry — a geo-tagged record of broken infrastructure (potholes, non-functional streetlights, flooded roads) with timestamps and photo evidence.
  • An RTI data pipeline — a template-driven workflow for filing Right to Information requests on local public spending and converting the responses into readable reporting.
  • A skills & livelihoods guild — a peer-to-peer learning structure where contributors build real features for the other two tools while building a public portfolio.

Sister project

Aether OS — Friction Engine

A separate, more developed project that measures and visualises "friction" — the cumulative time, paperwork, and repeat visits citizens spend navigating everyday government processes, from getting a certificate to filing a complaint. Built as an independent Next.js application.

It's a complementary effort to the demos above: where the civic-tech demos focus on documenting failures after the fact, the Friction Engine focuses on measuring the everyday cost of the system working "as designed."

Explore the Friction Engine →

Honest status: the three civic-tech demos are early prototypes running on mock data. None are connected to a live backend, a real reporting database, or an active RTI filing system yet. They exist to demonstrate what the tools would do and to invite contributors who want to help build the real versions. The Friction Engine is further along as a standalone application but remains an independent, experimental sub-project rather than a finished public service.

Nobody can dismiss a meme page as "lazy internet noise" forever — but nobody can dismiss a group of volunteers who shipped an open-source public dataset, either. Building real tools doesn't replace the work of reporting and advocacy; it gives that work something durable to point to. That's the bet this lab is making.
